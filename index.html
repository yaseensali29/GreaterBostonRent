Can You Afford Greater Boston?

Housing in Greater Boston has become harder to afford as rent prices continue to rise in Boston and nearby cities. This data story compares Boston, Quincy, Revere, and Medford to show how rent, income, and rent burden have changed over time.

Rent Has Been Rising Across Greater Boston

This chart shows how median monthly rent has changed over time in each city. Comparing the lines helps show whether rent is rising at a similar pace across Greater Boston or if some cities are becoming expensive faster than others.

The trend shows that rent has increased across all four cities, but the size and speed of the increase can vary by location.

Rent Compared to Income

Rent alone does not tell the full story. A city can have lower rent than Boston, but if income is also lower, residents may still struggle to afford housing. This chart compares median monthly rent with estimated monthly household income for each city in the latest year.

This comparison helps show the gap between what households earn each month and how much of that income may go toward rent.

Which City Is the Most Rent Burdened?

Rent burden shows the percentage of household income that goes toward rent. This is one of the clearest ways to measure affordability because it connects housing cost directly to income.

A higher rent burden means residents are spending a larger share of their income on housing, which can make a city less affordable even if the rent looks lower compared to Boston.

Conclusion

The data shows that affordability is not only about how high rent is, but also about whether income is keeping up. By comparing rent, income, and rent burden across Boston, Quincy, Revere, and Medford, this project shows how housing pressure affects both the city and nearby communities.

This project uses U.S. Census Bureau American Community Survey 5-year estimate data for Boston, Quincy, Revere, and Medford.

Data Sources

This project uses U.S. Census Bureau American Community Survey 5-year estimate data for Boston, Quincy, Revere, and Medford. Median gross rent and median household income were collected for each city, and rent burden was calculated as yearly rent divided by yearly household income. Rent burden was calculated using the formula: (median monthly rent × 12) ÷ median household income × 100.
